# Results and Discussion

## 1. Introduction and Experimental Context

This study investigates the efficacy of integrating analytical control-theoretic priors into deep reinforcement learning architectures. Specifically, we explore whether structuring the critic network's value function as $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$—where $\Phi(s)$ is a known, physically meaningful Lyapunov function and $f_\theta(s)$ is a learned neural network residual—can enhance sample efficiency, algorithmic stability, and final policy quality. The environment selected for this investigation is the classic continuous-control Gymnasium `Pendulum-v1` benchmark. The native reward function of the environment was entirely replaced by a Lyapunov-based reward shaping formulation, defined as the temporal difference in the system's mechanical energy: $R_t = \Phi(s_t) - \Phi(s_{t+1})$. 

To rigorously evaluate the proposed architectural modification, we conducted experiments across three distinct conditions. The first condition, the Vanilla Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) baseline, utilized the standard environment reward and a conventional, unstructured critic network. This baseline serves as an upper-bound reference for the solvability of the task. The second condition, Condition A (Direct Value Learning), employed the Lyapunov-based reward but maintained the standard unstructured critic, requiring the network to learn the value landscape entirely from empirical data. The third condition, Condition B (Structured Value Function), utilized both the Lyapunov reward and the proposed structural decomposition, initializing the value estimate with the analytic $\Phi(s)$ and learning only the residual corrections. All conditions were trained for 100,000 environment steps across five independent random seeds to account for the inherent stochasticity of deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Quantitative Performance Analysis

The empirical evaluation yielded a comprehensive set of quantitative metrics, revealing significant behavioral differences between the native reward formulation and the Lyapunov-shaped conditions, as well as nuanced distinctions between the structured and unstructured critic architectures.

The Vanilla SAC baseline demonstrated exceptional performance, achieving a final average reward (calculated over the last 10 episodes of training) of $-146.78 \pm 22.74$. In the context of the native Pendulum reward, this value indicates near-optimal behavior, as the agent successfully minimizes both positional deviation and control effort. This proficiency is further reflected in the final upright stability metric, where the Vanilla agent maintained the pendulum in the target upright region ($|\theta| < 0.1$ radians) for $64.78\% \pm 26.20\%$ of the evaluation timesteps. Furthermore, the baseline exhibited rapid convergence, reaching 90% of its maximum performance threshold in a mere $5525.53 \pm 534.95$ environment steps.

Conversely, the agents trained under the Lyapunov-based reward paradigm exhibited fundamentally different performance profiles. Condition A (Direct Value Learning) achieved a final average Lyapunov reward of $0.38 \pm 0.12$. While this positive value indicates that the agent successfully learned to decrease the system's energy over time, its final upright stability was remarkably poor, achieving only $10.46\% \pm 8.66\%$. The sample efficiency of Condition A was also severely degraded compared to the baseline, requiring $67767.77 \pm 8799.93$ steps to reach its 90% performance threshold.

Condition B (Structured Value Function) was designed to ameliorate these inefficiencies by providing a strong inductive bias. The empirical results show a slight improvement in the final average Lyapunov reward, reaching $0.47 \pm 0.09$. More notably, Condition B improved the mean sample efficiency, requiring $50030.03 \pm 25614.08$ steps to reach the convergence threshold—an average reduction of over 17,000 steps compared to Condition A. However, the final upright stability remained low at $7.06\% \pm 6.71\%$, indicating that the structural prior did not resolve the underlying behavioral misalignment caused by the reward formulation.

To determine the statistical significance of the observed differences between the structured approach (Condition B) and the direct learning approach (Condition A), we conducted independent two-sample t-tests. The analysis of the final performance yielded a t-statistic of $-1.295$ with a corresponding p-value of $0.231$. Similarly, the comparison of sample efficiency resulted in $t = 1.310$ and $p = 0.227$, while the final upright stability comparison yielded $t = 0.621$ and $p = 0.552$. In all cases, the p-values exceed the standard significance threshold of $\alpha = 0.05$. We must therefore conclude that, despite the observed improvements in the mean values for reward and sample efficiency, the high variance across the random seeds—particularly the massive standard deviation of 25,614 steps in Condition B's sample efficiency—renders the benefits of the structured value function statistically insignificant in this specific experimental setup. The structural initialization provides a strong early learning signal for some seeds, accelerating convergence, but it does not uniformly guarantee superior performance across all initializations.

## 3. Learning Dynamics and Temporal Stability

A deeper understanding of the training process can be derived from the temporal evolution of the agents' performance. The learning dynamics are visually captured in the multi-panel figure <code>data/learning_curves_and_stability_1_20260422_182852.png</code>.

The top-left panel of the figure illustrates the learning curves for Conditions A and B, plotting the cumulative Lyapunov reward against the number of environment steps. The solid and dashed lines represent the mean performance, while the shaded regions denote the standard deviation across the five seeds. Both conditions exhibit a characteristic reinforcement learning trajectory, starting from near-zero rewards and gradually ascending as the policy improves. However, Condition B demonstrates a noticeably steeper initial ascent during the first 40,000 steps. This visual evidence corroborates the hypothesis that initializing the critic with the analytic Lyapunov function reduces the initial search space, allowing the actor to receive more accurate gradient updates earlier in training. Nevertheless, as training progresses into the latter half of the 100,000 steps, the learning curve for Condition A catches up, and the shaded variance bands of the two conditions heavily overlap. This convergence explains the lack of statistical significance in the final performance metrics; the unstructured network eventually learns an adequate representation, neutralizing the early advantage of the structured approach.

The bottom-left panel displays the learning curve for the Vanilla SAC baseline. The trajectory here is starkly different, characterized by an extremely rapid ascent within the first 10,000 steps, followed by a stable plateau. The variance band is remarkably narrow, indicating highly consistent learning across all random seeds. This highlights the density and immediate utility of the native reward signal compared to the Lyapunov difference reward.

The right panel of the figure tracks the upright stability metric over the course of training. This plot is particularly revealing regarding the qualitative behavior of the learned policies. The Vanilla SAC agent's curve rapidly climbs to a high stability fraction, demonstrating that the native reward perfectly aligns with the human-interpretable goal of balancing the pendulum. In stark contrast, the curves for both Condition A and Condition B remain suppressed near the bottom of the y-axis throughout the entire training duration. Despite the agents successfully maximizing the Lyapunov reward (as seen in the top-left panel), this maximization does not translate into upright stability. The agents are learning to manipulate the system's energy, but they are not learning to balance. This critical divergence between reward maximization and task success will be analyzed in detail in Section 5.

## 4. Value Function Topography and Residual Analysis

To elucidate the internal representations learned by the critic networks and to validate the mechanics of the structured architecture, we generated high-resolution heatmaps of the value functions across a 2D grid of the state space ($\theta \in [-\pi, \pi], \dot\theta \in [-8, 8]$). These visualizations, derived from the median-performing seeds of each condition, are presented in <code>data/value_function_heatmaps_2_20260422_182852.png</code>.

Panel (a) establishes the ground truth by displaying the analytic Lyapunov function, $\Phi(s) = (1 - \cos\theta) + 0.5\dot\theta^2$. This function represents the mechanical energy of the pendulum relative to the upright position. The heatmap reveals a distinct, sharp global minimum at the center ($\theta=0, \dot\theta=0$), representing the target equilibrium. The energy increases sinusoidally along the horizontal axis (angle) and quadratically along the vertical axis (angular velocity), creating a clear, physically grounded basin of attraction.

Panel (b) illustrates the learned value function $V(s)$ for Condition A, where the network was forced to learn the topography entirely from the empirical reward signal $R_t = \Phi(s_t) - \Phi(s_{t+1})$. While the network successfully identifies the general location of the minimum near the center, the overall landscape is diffuse, relatively flat, and lacks the precise, sharp contours of the true mechanical energy. The direct learning approach struggles to perfectly reconstruct the underlying physical dynamics, likely due to the sparsity of state visitation in high-energy regions and the noise inherent in bootstrapping value estimates.

Panel (c) showcases the learned value function for Condition B, defined as $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$. The visual contrast with Condition A is striking. The resulting landscape in Panel (c) closely mirrors the analytic $\Phi(s)$ in Panel (a), maintaining the sharp gradients, the distinct central minimum, and the correct quadratic scaling at the edges of the state space. By explicitly embedding the physical structure into the critic's forward pass, Condition B ensures that the value estimates remain strictly grounded in the system's true energy landscape. This structural guarantee persists even in regions of the state space that the agent rarely visits, effectively solving the out-of-distribution generalization problem that plagues standard neural network critics.

Panel (d) provides the most profound insight into the learning process by isolating the learned residual, $f_\theta(s)$, plotted using a diverging colormap centered at zero. The residual network's objective is to capture the components of the true value function that deviate from the idealized analytic $\Phi(s)$. To interpret this heatmap, we must consider the mathematics of the discounted return. Given the reward $R_t = \Phi(s_t) - \Phi(s_{t+1})$ and a discount factor $\gamma < 1$, the true value function is the expected discounted sum of future rewards:
$V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E} [ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t (\Phi(s_t) - \Phi(s_{t+1})) ]$
By expanding the telescoping sum, this resolves to:
$V^\pi(s) = \Phi(s_0) - (1 - \gamma) \mathbb{E} [ \sum_{t=1}^\infty \gamma^{t-1} \Phi(s_t) ]$
Because the structured critic is defined as $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$, it mathematically follows that the optimal residual must converge to:
$f_\theta(s) \approx -(1 - \gamma) \mathbb{E} [ \sum_{t=1}^\infty \gamma^{t-1} \Phi(s_t) ]$
Since $\Phi(s)$ is strictly positive definite, the theoretical residual must be predominantly negative. Furthermore, the magnitude of this negative residual should be largest in states where the agent cannot rapidly dissipate energy—specifically, states with high angular velocity where the required deceleration exceeds the environment's torque limits ($u \in [-2, 2]$).

The heatmap in Panel (d) perfectly corroborates this theoretical derivation. The residual is almost entirely negative (indicated by the blue hues), and the most intense negative corrections are localized in the high-energy corners of the state space (large $|\dot\theta|$ and $|\theta| \approx \pm \pi$). In these regions, the unconstrained analytic $\Phi(s)$ overestimates the agent's ability to reach the goal due to actuator saturation. The neural network successfully learns to apply a negative correction, effectively penalizing states from which the agent cannot quickly recover. This confirms that the residual network is not merely memorizing noise, but is actively learning the complex, non-linear effects of control limits and temporal discounting that are absent from the idealized physical equations.

## 5. Discussion, Limitations, and Future Directions

The synthesis of the quantitative metrics and the qualitative heatmap analysis presents a complex picture of Lyapunov-based reward shaping and structured value functions.

The primary hypothesis—that structuring the critic as $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$ improves learning—is supported by the representational quality of the value function, but not by the statistical significance of the performance metrics. The structured architecture undeniably produces a more accurate, physically grounded value landscape and successfully isolates the non-ideal dynamics into an interpretable residual. However, in a relatively low-dimensional environment like the Pendulum, standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms are sufficiently powerful to overcome the lack of structural priors given enough time. The true potential of this architecture likely lies in high-dimensional robotics or complex fluid dynamics, where learning the baseline physics from scratch is computationally prohibitive, and embedding an approximate analytical model could yield massive, statistically significant gains in sample efficiency.

The most critical limitation uncovered by this study is the failure of the Lyapunov difference reward ($R_t = \Phi(s_t) - \Phi(s_{t+1})$) to induce the desired upright stabilization behavior, as evidenced by the sub-15% stability fractions compared to the ~65% achieved by the native reward. This failure can be attributed to two primary factors.

First, the reward formulation creates a telescoping sum. An agent maximizing the undiscounted return simply seeks to minimize the final state's energy relative to the initial state, largely ignoring the trajectory. Even with discounting, the agent may find local optima, such as continuously swinging the pendulum to generate alternating positive and negative rewards, rather than committing to the unstable equilibrium where the step-by-step reward is exactly zero.

Second, the scale of the Lyapunov reward near the equilibrium is extremely small. The Soft Actor-Critic algorithm maximizes a combination of expected reward and policy entropy. Near the upright position ($\theta \approx 0, \dot\theta \approx 0$), the differences in $\Phi(s)$ become infinitesimally small. Consequently, the entropy bonus dominates the optimization objective, causing the agent to inject noise into its actions. This stochasticity inevitably pushes the pendulum out of the delicate equilibrium, leading to the poor stability metrics observed. The native reward, which heavily penalizes deviations with a much larger magnitude (up to -16 per step), effectively overpowers the entropy bonus and enforces strict stabilization.

Future research should address these limitations by decoupling the structural critic architecture from the specific reward formulation used in this study. The structured value function $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$ could be utilized in conjunction with the native environment reward, using $\Phi(s)$ purely as an initialization prior rather than tying it to a shaped reward. Alternatively, if reward shaping is desired, it should be implemented using strictly policy-invariant potential-based shaping ($F(s, s') = \gamma \Phi(s') - \Phi(s)$) to ensure that the optimal policy remains unchanged while still providing dense guidance. Ultimately, this study demonstrates that while embedding physical equations into neural networks yields highly interpretable and accurate representations, the alignment between the mathematical reward formulation and the practical control objective remains the most critical determinant of reinforcement learning success.
