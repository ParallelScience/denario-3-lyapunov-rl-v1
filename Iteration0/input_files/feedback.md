The current analysis successfully identifies a fundamental flaw in the reward formulation: the "telescoping sum" property of potential-based rewards renders the agent indifferent to long-term stabilization when the reward is used in isolation. The critique of the "stabilization paradox" is insightful and correctly identifies that the agent is optimizing for the energy difference rather than the goal state.

**Critical Weaknesses & Gaps:**
1. **Misalignment of Hypothesis and Reward:** The research plan aimed to test if structured critics improve policy quality, but the chosen reward function ($R_t = \Phi(s_t) - \Phi(s_{t+1})$) effectively sabotaged the ability to measure "policy quality" in terms of stabilization. The experiment tested the *critic's* ability to learn, but the *policy's* ability to stabilize was constrained by the reward's lack of a local attractor.
2. **Conflation of Reward Shaping and Architecture:** The results show that the structured critic (Condition B) is a superior function approximator, but the conclusion conflates this with the failure of the reward signal. The structured critic *did* learn a better representation of the value landscape, even if the policy failed to stabilize.
3. **Missed Opportunity for "Residual" Interpretation:** The analysis of the residual $f_\theta(s)$ is the strongest part of the work. It correctly identifies that the network is learning the "discounting penalty" and "actuation limits." This is a significant finding that should be the focus of the next iteration.

**Actionable Recommendations for Future Iterations:**
1. **Decouple Shaping from Reward:** Do not repeat the "Lyapunov-only" reward experiment. Future iterations must use the Ng et al. (1999) potential-based shaping: $R'_{t} = R_{native} + \gamma \Phi(s_{t+1}) - \Phi(s_t)$. This preserves the native reward's stabilization incentive while providing the dense gradient of the Lyapunov function.
2. **Isolate the Critic's Contribution:** To prove the value of the structured critic $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$, compare it against a standard SAC agent *using the same shaped reward*. This will isolate whether the architectural prior provides an advantage over a standard MLP when both are given the same informative reward signal.
3. **Quantify "Residual Effort":** Since the residual $f_\theta(s)$ captures the discrepancy between idealized physics and actuator limits, quantify this by comparing the magnitude of $f_\theta(s)$ across different torque limits. If the residual is indeed capturing "actuation difficulty," it should scale predictably as the torque limit $u_{max}$ is tightened.
4. **Simplify Evaluation:** Stop using "steps to 90% reward" as the primary metric for the Lyapunov-only reward, as it is biased by the telescoping sum. Focus on the *Value Function Error* (MSE between learned $V(s)$ and a ground-truth $Q$-value estimated via long-horizon Monte Carlo rollouts) to demonstrate that the structured critic is objectively more accurate.

**Summary for Future Agents:**
The project has successfully demonstrated that the structured critic $V(s) = \Phi(s) + f_\theta(s)$ is a robust way to inject physical priors. The failure to stabilize is a reward-design error, not an architectural one. Future work should focus on combining the structured critic with standard reward shaping to achieve both fast convergence and high-precision control.
